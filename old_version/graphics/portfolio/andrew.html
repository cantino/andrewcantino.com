Final Porfolio
Andrew Cantino

This is Andrew's final portfolio for Computer Graphics at Swarthmore College. On all images, click for a larger version.


Multiple Andrews bluescreened onto a photograph.
I removed the blue background from the original image, and then superimposed the resulting image over the photograph. To get the Andrew behind the mountain, I bluescreened the sky out of the photo and then layered a bluescreened version of Andrew between the sky and the foreground of the photo.


Julia Set fractal from (-.1, -.5) to (1, .1)


Microsoft Paint would have been faster.
This image demonstrates the use of 2D polygons, circles, ellipses, and normal & gradient flood fills.


Totally cool gradient train!
This image demonstrates the use of 2D polygons, circles, and gradient fills.


What's AC stand for?
To make this image I used flood fills, scanline fills, gradient fills, circles, and lots of polygons.


Space Warp!
This image was made with alpha blending, and procedural programming of our hierarchical modeling system.


Balloons Go By!
This image further demonstrates the flexibility of our hierarchical modeling system. I only had to model this balloon once, then wrote a simple particle engine to animate multiple balloons rising into the sky.


Pyramids gone CRAZY!
This image demonstrates our 3D modeling, transformation, and rendering pipeline. It was rendered using our Z-buffer.


Enterprise Orbiting 2.0
This image uses just about everything that we have written: parametric surfaces, 3D modeling, transformations, rendering, and lighting.


Spheres with shadows, oh my!
This image demonstrates shadow rays, in which we shoot a ray from every rendered pixel to each light source, looking for intersections (that imply shadows).


Eagle with 33,000 triangles.
This eagle was rendered with our system. It was imported using an ASE importer that we wrote.
(Original: 3dcafe.com)


Torus hanging from a point.
Demonstrates our physics model. Each vertex in the torus has a mass and is connected to the other vertices by springs.


Wiggly sheet.
A sheet fixed at the top, hanging in Earth's gravity, is suddenly displaced upwards from the bottom. Demonstrates our vector physics model.
